# Center-Mist Maintenance Schedule

## Overview

Center-Mist Cooling requires more than a water tank and mist module.

Because the system may release fine droplets near people, roads, vehicles, and urban spaces, maintenance scheduling is a core safety and hygiene requirement.

This document provides representative maintenance intervals for conceptual design discussion. It is not a certified maintenance standard.

All intervals listed here are initial design guidelines that require field validation. Actual intervals must be determined by engineering testing, field data, manufacturer design, legal requirements, hygiene standards, and local climate.

---

## Why Maintenance Scheduling Matters

A mist system that releases fine water droplets near pedestrians, road users, and public spaces carries hygiene and inhalation safety obligations that go beyond ordinary mechanical maintenance.

Key reasons maintenance scheduling matters:

- Stagnant water in a tank at elevated temperature can support biofilm and microbial growth
- Poor water quality can reduce mist output, clog nozzles and ultrasonic modules, and create inhalation hazards
- Filters can saturate or clog, reducing flow and increasing pump load
- UV-C lamps have finite service lives and lose effectiveness before visible failure
- Ion-generating electrodes degrade and may lose output without obvious signs
- Sensors drift and may give false readings if not periodically calibrated or replaced
- Gravity-fed recovery lines can accumulate debris and develop flow resistance over time
- Maintenance needs vary significantly by climate, water quality, and operating hours

Skipping maintenance does not simply reduce comfort. In a mist system, it can compromise the health and safety of people exposed to the mist output.

## Maintenance Factors

Actual maintenance intervals depend on:

- ambient temperature
- humidity
- dust and sand load
- water mineral content
- refill method
- tank material
- filter design
- ultrasonic module design
- daily operating hours
- vehicle vibration
- depot access
- sensor reliability
- hygiene regulations
- inhalation safety requirements

No single schedule applies to all climates and all deployments. The intervals in this document are representative starting points for conceptual design discussion only.

## Climate-Based Maintenance Categories

| Category | Example environment | Main risk |
|---|---|---|
| Dry and dusty climate | Desert roads, Middle East dry corridors, construction zones | Dust, sand, clogging, mineral scaling, short filter life |
| Hot urban climate | Heat-island roads, taxis, buses, logistics vehicles | Warm stagnant water, biofilm, odor, high use hours |
| Rainy climate | Regions with regular rainfall and recovered water supply | Organic matter, storage contamination, variable water quality |
| Humid climate | Coastal or tropical cities | Lower evaporation efficiency, microbial growth, tank hygiene |
| Depot-managed fleet | Buses, logistics, airport, port vehicles | High operating hours but easier inspection and refill control |

## Dry and Dusty Climate Scenario

In dry and dusty regions, direct tank refill may be more practical than open recovered-water supply.

However, dust, sand, and mineral particles can shorten maintenance intervals.

Recommended design assumptions include:

- sealed water tank
- filtered refill water
- replaceable filter cartridges
- quick-access tank drain
- sediment trap
- clogging detection
- short inspection cycles
- maintenance alerts

In Middle East or desert-climate deployments, ambient temperatures during summer operation may significantly accelerate biofilm growth and mineral scaling. Maintenance intervals that appear adequate in temperate climates may need to be substantially reduced.

---

## Rainy Climate / Dual-Supply Scenario

In regions with year-round rainfall, a dual-supply system may be useful:

1. recovered rainwater or drain water supply
2. direct tank refill backup

This can reduce tap-water demand, but recovered water requires filtration, storage hygiene, sediment control, and microbial risk management.

Organic matter, leaves, dust, and particulates in recovered water may increase biofilm risk and shorten filter life compared to tap-water supply. Water quality monitoring is more critical than in direct-refill systems.

---

## Urban Fleet / Depot Scenario

Depot-managed vehicles — buses, logistics trucks, airport shuttles, port service vehicles — operate many hours per day and return to a fixed location for overnight service.

This creates both higher total-use maintenance needs and better depot-access opportunities.

Recommended design assumptions for depot fleets:

- end-of-shift tank inspection
- daily filter check
- depot-side top-up and water quality monitoring
- scheduled full cleans aligned with vehicle maintenance cycles
- sensor calibration coordinated with workshop schedules
- maintenance log requirements per vehicle

High daily operating hours may require daily filter replacement in dusty or mineral-rich water environments.

---

## Direct Tank Refill Scenario

When the vehicle is refilled from a controlled source — tap water, filtered service water, depot water supply — water quality is more predictable than in recovered-water systems.

Even so, direct-refill tanks still require:

- regular drain and rinse cycles
- sediment check after refill
- biofilm inspection at scheduled intervals
- filter condition check
- mineral scaling check in hard-water regions

Direct refill does not eliminate hygiene requirements. It reduces some variables while leaving others unchanged.

---

## Water Quality and Hygiene Checks

Water quality checks are necessary because mist droplets released from a vehicle may be inhaled by pedestrians, road users, vehicle occupants, and maintenance workers.

Minimum recommended checks include:

- visual inspection: odor, color, cloudiness, visible particles
- turbidity monitoring where sensor is fitted
- conductivity or total-dissolved-solids check in mineral-risk regions
- pH check where ion-assisted hygiene control is in use
- biofilm visual inspection of accessible tank interior and filter surfaces
- sediment check at tank drain

These checks are representative. Specific monitoring requirements depend on local water quality standards, hygiene regulations, and inhalation safety requirements for mist-release systems.

---

## Filter and Anti-Clogging Checks

Filters are among the highest-maintenance components in a mist system.

In dry or dusty climates, coarse filters may need daily inspection. In rainy-climate dual-supply systems, fine filters may need more frequent attention due to organic matter and variable source water quality.

Filter checks should confirm:

- no visible blockage or heavy particle accumulation
- normal flow and mist output (no unexplained reduction)
- no bypass leakage around filter seal
- no evidence of biofilm on filter surface or housing
- filter condition is within replacement threshold

Filter replacement intervals should be defined by the manufacturer or determined by field testing. The representative schedule above is a starting point only.

---

## UV-C / Ion-Assisted Hygiene Checks

UV-C lamps and ion-generating electrodes are auxiliary hygiene support units. They are not guaranteed sterilization systems.

Regular checks should confirm:

- UV-C lamp is operational and has not reached end of rated service life
- UV-C lamp output is not reduced by mineral scaling on the lamp sleeve or housing
- ion-generating electrodes are not excessively degraded
- ion concentration is within the intended safe operating range
- water clarity is sufficient for UV-C penetration
- no abnormal odor or color that may indicate treatment failure

UV-C effectiveness is significantly reduced by high turbidity or mineral content. If water quality degrades, UV-C cannot compensate for poor filtration or inadequate tank cleaning.

Ion concentrations that are too high may affect water chemistry and require validation for inhalation safety before deployment.

---

## Gravity-Fed Recovery Line Checks

The gravity-fed micro hydropower recovery line should be inspected as part of the water circulation system.

Maintenance should confirm:

- no debris accumulation
- no excessive hydraulic resistance
- no fouling of the micro turbine
- no leakage
- no pump-load increase caused by poor placement
- stable return or overflow flow

This component is not a primary power source. It only recovers a small amount of otherwise lost gravity-flow energy.

If this component increases hydraulic resistance in the return line, it may cause the circulation pump to work harder than the recovered power justifies. In that case, it should be repositioned or removed from the system.

---

## Sensor and Alert System Checks

Sensors and alert systems are the first line of maintenance awareness in a mist system.

Checks should confirm:

- water-level sensor is reading correctly and has not drifted
- water-quality sensors (turbidity, conductivity, pH where fitted) are within calibration range
- clogging detection is functional
- filter-saturation alerts are triggering at the correct threshold
- temperature sensors (ambient, tank, road) are reading correctly
- maintenance alert LEDs and control system warnings are functional
- no sensor that has been inactive for an extended period without a recent calibration check

Sensor calibration intervals depend on sensor type and manufacturer recommendation. The weekly check in the representative schedule is a starting point, not a validated interval.

---

## Field Validation Requirements

The representative schedule in this document is an initial design guideline. It does not replace field validation.

Validation requirements include:

- climate-specific testing under real temperature, humidity, and dust conditions
- microbial growth testing under local water quality and ambient temperature
- filter life testing with representative refill water and operating hours
- mineral scaling tests under regional hard-water conditions
- vibration and mechanical durability testing under vehicle operating conditions
- inhalation safety review for mist released from the local water supply
- UV-C effectiveness testing at local water turbidity and mineral content
- ion concentration safety testing if ion-assisted hygiene control is used
- maintenance cost evaluation per vehicle and per fleet
- comparison of observed intervals vs. representative intervals in this document

Actual maintenance intervals must be based on this field data, not on the representative schedule alone.

---

## Limitations

This schedule is illustrative.

It does not replace manufacturer maintenance manuals, legal inspection requirements, water hygiene regulations, or field-tested engineering standards.

Real deployment requires climate-specific validation, microbial testing, mineral scaling tests, vibration testing, filter-life testing, inhalation safety review, and maintenance cost evaluation.

The intervals shown depend heavily on climate, water quality, dust load, operation hours, and local regulation. They may be shorter or longer than shown depending on actual conditions.

No performance, hygiene safety, or regulatory compliance claim is made for any interval listed in this document.
