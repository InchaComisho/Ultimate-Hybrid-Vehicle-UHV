# Human Heat Stress Interface

This document explains how UHV cooling concepts may connect to human thermal comfort and heat-stress research concepts. It does not make medical claims and does not claim that UHV has been validated to prevent illness or guarantee safety. Detailed human-subject, urban-climate, and transport-safety validation would be required before any public deployment.

## Why Road-Level Cooling May Matter

People near roads, bus stops, sidewalks, and transit corridors experience heat from several sources at once:

* air temperature near road level
* radiant heat from asphalt and concrete
* heat emitted by vehicles and air-conditioning exhaust
* low wind or stagnant air around buildings
* humidity that limits sweat evaporation
* direct solar radiation

UHV focuses on the road-adjacent environment because that is where pedestrians, cyclists, transit riders, drivers, and outdoor workers may encounter high thermal stress during daily mobility.

## Air Temperature Near Road Level

Air close to asphalt can be warmer than standard weather-station air temperature, especially in full sun and low wind. A moving or parked vehicle with a controlled external cooling module could be studied as a local airflow and evaporative-cooling source.

The relevant measurement zone is not only the vehicle cabin. It includes:

* curbside air around bus stops
* sidewalk edges beside traffic lanes
* loading zones
* airport and port work zones
* transit corridors with repeated vehicle movement

## Radiant Heat From Asphalt

Human thermal comfort is affected by radiant heat, not only air temperature. Hot asphalt can emit and reflect heat toward legs and lower body regions. A UHV field test should therefore measure both air temperature and road surface temperature.

Representative sensors may include:

* ambient temperature sensors at pedestrian height
* road surface infrared temperature sensors
* relative humidity sensors
* wind speed and direction sensors
* solar radiation or shade condition notes

## Humidity and Evaporative Cooling

Evaporative cooling works by converting liquid water into vapor. This can remove heat from air, but it also adds water vapor. In dry climates, this may support local cooling if droplets evaporate before reaching the ground. In humid climates, evaporation slows and additional moisture can make perceived heat worse.

UHV mist control should therefore consider:

* relative humidity
* droplet size
* airflow and wind direction
* pedestrian density
* road wetness
* visibility
* whether the space is open or enclosed

## Perceived Temperature and Heat Stress

Perceived temperature depends on temperature, humidity, radiation, wind, clothing, activity level, and exposure time. UHV should not be evaluated by air temperature alone. A validation program may compare multiple indicators, but any human-comfort interpretation should be cautious and reviewed by qualified specialists.

UHV could reduce local thermal stress only if it reduces heat exposure without creating offsetting risks such as wet surfaces, visibility loss, excessive humidity, contaminated water exposure, or added vehicle safety hazards.

## Pedestrian Exposure Zones

Candidate validation zones include:

* bus stops
* sidewalks next to bus routes
* station plazas
* airport apron service areas
* port logistics yards
* construction vehicle zones
* outdoor queue areas

Pedestrian exposure should be measured before and after UHV operation. The system should avoid spraying people directly unless a specific approved test protocol allows it.

## Why Excessive Humidity or Wet Surfaces Must Be Avoided

Mist cooling is useful only when the water evaporates safely and efficiently. Excessive humidity can reduce human evaporative cooling from sweating. Wet surfaces can increase slip risk for pedestrians and traction risk for vehicles. Dense mist can reduce visibility.

For this reason, UHV validation should include conservative shutoff conditions:

* high humidity
* rain
* visible road wetting
* poor visibility
* low wind in enclosed zones
* high pedestrian density
* unsafe sensor readings

## Validation Direction

A human heat-stress interface study should be designed as an environmental and safety validation process, not as a health guarantee. Recommended outputs include measured temperature, humidity, surface temperature, wind, wetting, visibility, water use, and qualitative comfort observations under approved test conditions.
